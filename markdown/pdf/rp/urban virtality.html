Measure the "Pattern Language":

Evaluate the relationship between designing semantic features and urban vitality.

 

Research Gap

The ‘Urban Vitality’ has become a classic issue in urban planning, especially since "The Charter of Machu Picchu" ("The Charter of Machu Picchu", 1979) established the centrality of "human" in the field of urban planning and design.

In recent years, many literatures have used quantitative methods to discuss the relationship between vitality, physical activities ,walk willingness and the built environment attributes. Among the building attributes ,Greenery and "5D characteristics." were often considered as important variables. (Cervero & Kockelman, 1997; L. Chen et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2017, 2018; Lu, 2019) Further, some classical urban planning and design theories about vitality began to be evaluated, such as Jacobs' classic advice on neighborhood vitality. (Huang et al., 2022)

However, there are still many empirical "urban planning classical guidelines" that have yet to be evaluated , because they are "designing semantic features" different from "image elements of shallow levels " . The designing semantic features always use the abstract “pattern language” such as "legibility","distinct features", "unity of opposites", "human scale", "harmonious colors", "enclosed boundary", "visual rhythms","visual orders" and so on. (Alexander, 1977; Gehl, 2013; Lennard et al., 1987; Lynch, 1984; Marcus & Francis, 1997; Smith et al., 1997)

So far, the most widely recognized work in this field is that Ewing conducted a systematic review and verification of these "walkable friendly environment characteristics", and obtained five most important quality characteristics related to walking vitality: Enclosure, Human Scale, Complexity, Imagability and Transparency(Ewing et al., 2013). However, the results were based on small-scale photographs and expert ratings. There are still gaps in large-scale and in-depth evaluation.

In recent years, deep learning methods have provided an opportunity to explore deep image features . In fact, these deep image features have been applied to identify urban elements , tree cate gories, housing price and vote prediction in some research.(M. Chen et al., 2022; Gebru et al., 2017; Zhang et al., n.d., 2018, 2019；Liu et al., 2023).

So, is it possible to recognize the "Architectural pattern language" of traditional design disciplines? Although these "deep features" are difficult to measure, these abstract "Architectural Pattern Languages"(Alexander, 1977) have long been considered central to the discipline of urban planning and design(彭一刚, 1998). And this research will be the first work to evaluate the relationship between image designing semantic features and urban vitality.

There are several key difficulties have yet to be solved in the variables calculation:

  • It requests for a well-trained neural networks model to recognize the "Imageability"in images. For example, we can train a model that recognize the "imagable elements" such as "non-rectangular architecture" and sculptures.

  • The result of Scene semantics have hundreds of categories. Therefore, we should first select a subset of 365 scene features that are important and relevent to urban vitality.

     

2. Regression Analysis

We can use two models: Random Forest and Geographically Weighted Regression(or GTWR) model to investigate the relationship between vitality and building environment attributes.
